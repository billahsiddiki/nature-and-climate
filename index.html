Cover Story • International Law • Issue One

Justice for a Warming World

Dr. Payam Akhavan Professor of International Law, Massey College, University of Toronto Counsel to Bangladesh, ICJ & ITLOS Climate Proceedings

Dr. Payam Akhavan is a globally renowned international lawyer and a leading advocate for climate justice. As a Member of the Permanent Court of Arbitration at The Hague, he is at the forefront of efforts to recognise climate harm as a matter of international law. In this interview, Dr. Akhavan discusses the ICJ's landmark advisory opinion, the legal rights of vulnerable nations, and why the fight against climate change is ultimately a fight for global justice and human dignity.

  • What are the key legal obligations for states outlined by the ICJ in its advisory opinion on climate change?

    The primary obligation stems from the principle of transboundary harm, which prohibits a state from using its territory in a manner that causes significant harm to others. Consequently, the foremost duty is to mitigate greenhouse gas emissions. This obligation falls most heavily on developed nations, reflecting the principle of common but differentiated responsibilities.

    Significantly, the Court specifically addresses the production, consumption, and subsidization of fossil fuels, stating that these can constitute internationally wrongful acts in appropriate circumstances. By doing so, the ICJ explicitly outlines the obligation of states to transition away from fossil fuels.

  • In what ways does the advisory opinion elevate the Paris Agreement's 1.5°C target from an aspirational goal to a legally relevant standard?

    This is a crucial point. The Court held that the uncontested scientific evidence — which shows an exponential increase in the risk of catastrophic harm beyond a 1.5-degree Celsius temperature rise — means that the obligation of due diligence must be measured against this target.

    Therefore, the duty to exercise due diligence to prevent significant harm is no longer an abstract concept. It is concretely defined in relation to the 1.5-degree goal. States must exercise due diligence by ensuring that their contributions to greenhouse gas mitigation align with staying within this temperature limit.

  • How does the ICJ link climate change to human rights obligations under international law?

    The ICJ observed that climate change impacts fundamental human rights, including the right to life, the right to health, and the right to a healthy environment — rights already recognised in various UN treaties and declarations.

    However, a critical challenge remains: human rights law is predominantly territorial. For a country like Bangladesh, which contributes negligibly to global emissions yet suffers disproportionate impacts, this territorial focus is insufficient. The principle of transboundary harm more directly addresses the obligations states owe one another in such circumstances.

  • What is the significance of the ICJ recognising transboundary environmental harm and state responsibility for private actors?

    The significance lies not in creating new law, but in applying established principles to the novel context of climate change. The principle of transboundary harm is over a century old, and it is already well-established that a state has a duty to regulate private actors within its territory to prevent harm to other states.

    The pivotal step taken by the ICJ is to explicitly apply these long-standing doctrines to greenhouse gas emissions, thereby clarifying that states are legally responsible for controlling emissions from private industry within their borders.

  • How might the ICJ opinion strengthen Bangladesh's position in international negotiations and leverage for climate finance?

    As a low-lying coastal state that is especially vulnerable to climate change, Bangladesh's pleadings were followed attentively by the Court. This advisory opinion fundamentally strengthens the position of Bangladesh in two key ways.

    First, in forums like the COP negotiations, Bangladesh can now authoritatively state that the long-sought goals of meaningful mitigation, adaptation, and compensation for loss and damages are not merely political requests but are, in fact, binding obligations under international law.

    Second, beyond diplomacy, the opinion provides a legal foundation for potential future contentious proceedings against major emitter states. It shifts the paradigm from vague, voluntary promises to the realm of concrete legal duties, empowering vulnerable nations in their advocacy for climate finance and technical support.

  • How does the ICJ's advisory opinion address the principle of Common but Differentiated Responsibilities, and what are its implications for Bangladesh?

    The ICJ confirms the CBDR principle, which was already established in the 1992 UN Framework Convention on Climate Change. It further clarifies that this principle places the greatest burden on developed states, due to their largest historical carbon footprint. This includes their obligations regarding technology transfer and other forms of assistance to less developed and Least Developed Countries.

    However, it is important to note that for countries such as China, India, Brazil, and Saudi Arabia, the CBDR principle does not absolve them of responsibility. They are not "off the hook" and must also take stringent measures to mitigate their carbon footprints.

  • How can Bangladesh's legal system incorporate these international climate obligations into its domestic law?

    Even without direct applicability, it would certainly be incumbent upon the parliament and other governmental bodies — which contributed significantly to the opinion — to study it and adopt new laws and regulations to reflect this jurisprudence in domestic law, whether through legislation or judicial application.

    I would also note that Bangladesh has already made significant progress in moving toward green energy and is a progressive force on climate change. I am particularly pleased that there is a vibrant civil society in Bangladesh that has been engaged on climate change issues, and such engagement will only increase in importance.

The Fight for Climate Justice

Farzana Faruk Jhumu Youth Advisor on Climate Change to the Secretary-General, United Nations

Farzana Faruk Jhumu is a prominent climate justice activist whose work spans global climate diplomacy and community-centred solutions. She brings critical attention to the lived realities of those most affected by the climate crisis and advocates for a future where climate justice, equity, and accountability are non-negotiable principles in global decision-making.

  • Climate change is reshaping our seasons and routines. How are these changes affecting everyday life in Bangladesh?

    On a personal level, when I was young, my parents and grandparents used to tell us how distinctly they could feel the change of seasons. Bangladesh is, after all, the only country that officially has six seasons. But today, I feel like I can only experience different forms of summer. From this, it's clear that the change is quite significant.

    While working with vulnerable communities, I often hear that although people have become better at adaptation, they're not getting enough time to adjust. The prediction period is too short, and people are in a constant struggle to cope. Many vulnerable individuals don't even understand what their rights are or when those rights are being violated, because they are always in survival mode.

  • With climate change uprooting lives and deepening hardships especially for women, what first-hand experiences stand out to you?

    One of my experiences was in YouthNet Char in Kurigram, where a summit was organised. A young girl, around 16 or 17 years old, came there with her 3-year-old child — essentially a child carrying another child. The main concern we felt was: why are girls marrying so early? What we learned was that since there are no proper educational opportunities, families believe there's no reason to keep girls at home, so they marry them off early.

    I had a similar experience in Satkhira, where I met a 17-year-old girl who also had a 2-year-old baby. Her mother told me that they arranged her marriage early because they live in a saline-affected area — many women face severe menstrual health issues. So, in an attempt to secure their daughters' future, they are unknowingly destroying it — and this entire situation is a direct consequence of climate change.

    In the long run, the only real solution lies in mitigation. You can't tell a woman to "adapt" to such suffering. Adaptation in this context means sacrificing her own body. The only sustainable path forward is to minimise and mitigate the impacts of climate change.

  • How effectively are women's voices being included in global climate policy and negotiation spaces?

    I volunteer with the Women and Gender Constituency under the UNFCCC COP process. If we look back to 1995, during the Beijing Conference of CSW, climate change wasn't even recognised as a major issue within the feminist movement. But now, as we approach Beijing+30, it has become a key agenda item.

    Within the UNFCCC, there is now a dedicated Gender Action Plan — something that didn't exist a decade ago. Feminist advocacy has also helped bring new perspectives, such as recognising women's unpaid care work as part of the global economy. It's estimated that the world loses around $5.7 trillion annually due to unpaid care work, most of which is done by women.

    However, in many countries, women's empowerment is still treated narrowly. When it comes to women's health, safety, and security, particularly during disasters, we still lack women in decision-making roles. This must change if we want true gender-sensitive climate action.

  • What gaps still exist in Bangladesh's climate and gender policies?

    From a feminist and policy perspective, Bangladesh has made commendable progress. We have a Gender Action Plan — something many countries lack — but it needs updating to better reflect current realities. After sustained advocacy, NDC 3.0 now includes much stronger gender and inclusion components.

    The main challenge, however, lies in coordination and implementation. Many of our policies remain disconnected. For Bangladesh to assert global leadership, we must demonstrate not just need, but capacity, transparency, and accountability — proving that we can use climate finance effectively and deliver results.

  • Do you think we have sufficient climate warnings to take timely action?

    In terms of community preparedness, Bangladesh has a robust early warning system. Under the UN's Early Warnings for All initiative, Bangladesh is often highlighted as a country capable of managing local-level responses effectively with limited resources. The Cyclone Preparedness Programme is a great example — it's a community-based model that ensures information access through radio, local news, and volunteer networks.

    However, when it comes to long-term climate trends, the situation is quite different. We often fail to understand or communicate the long-term nature of climate change, so communities don't always have access to that kind of information. Bangladesh needs to focus more on adaptation — particularly by doubling adaptation finance and ensuring that both adaptation and loss-and-damage funds reach the most vulnerable communities quickly and transparently.

Strengthening Climate Policy & Accountability

Shaheen Anam Executive Director, Manusher Jonno Foundation • Honorary MBE

Shaheen Anam is a leading national voice on climate justice, governance, and gender equality. She speaks on why climate resilience must be treated as a central pillar of national development — not as a separate environmental agenda — and why without transparency and accountability, climate finance will never reach those who need it most.

  • How is climate change currently impacting Bangladesh's coastal and riverine communities?

    Climate change is an existential challenge for Bangladesh, one that is approaching fast with dangerous consequences. Unfortunately, commitments to keep Greenhouse Gas emissions to 2.5 levels are not being met as many countries are not cooperating to limit use of fossil fuel. Bangladesh is facing the consequences of the irresponsibility of larger nations.

    A recent global study revealed that in the eastern coastal area of Bangladesh, saline water intrusion is rising much more quickly than elsewhere. Due to sea level rise, millions of people are already at risk of losing their shelter, crops, and means of livelihood. Every day thousands of people who have lost their homestead and means of livelihood due to river erosion, floods, salinity etc are migrating to cities in search of work. Our cities do not have the means to provide decent housing and employment.

  • How effective are Bangladesh's existing policies in building real climate resilience?

    Building climate change resilience effectively requires more than just policy promises — it demands massive investment in both physical and social infrastructure. Clean water, safe housing, reliable healthcare, and improved community security must become national priorities.

    The foundation of this effort lies in financing and accountability. Securing sufficient funds for adaptation and mitigation is important, but ensuring transparency in how that money is used is even more vital. Too often, financing reaches projects but fails to reach the affected people. Clear monitoring systems, community participation, and independent audits should be embedded in every major climate investment initiative.

    Equally critical is the gender dimension: women remain severely underrepresented in climate policy and decision-making processes. Without women at the table, our planning lacks inclusivity. Climate change is no longer an environmental issue alone — it is a national development challenge that demands foresight, inclusivity, and unwavering political will.

  • Women are often on the frontlines of climate stress. In what ways are they disproportionately affected in Bangladesh?

    Women are disproportionately affected by climate change because of their traditional roles and daily responsibilities — fetching water, cooking, collecting firewood, and tending to livestock. In many coastal and drought-prone areas, a woman may have to walk two or more kilometres just to fetch a single pot of water. This prolonged exposure to saline or contaminated water leads to serious health problems, particularly affecting their reproductive health.

    When women spend hours collecting water or fuel, their safety is constantly at risk. Young girls face an equally grim reality. As families lose income and stability, many girls are forced to drop out of school, and early marriage becomes a means of survival. In every crisis, women are affected in ways that are distinct, complex, and far more severe. Addressing these realities demands a gender-just approach that places women's safety, health, and empowerment at the very centre of climate action.

  • What changes in public awareness or behaviour do you believe are essential for Bangladesh to build a truly resilient, climate-conscious future?

    My expectation is for everyone to realise how serious the threat of climate change is. We must keep in mind that climate change is not coming — it has already come! The changing patterns of excessive rain, heat and floods, plus rising salinity and sea level rise should remind us that the threat to lives of millions is real and imminent.

    Furthermore, our education curriculum should include in-depth chapters on climate change and its impacts. Children have to be educated about this very real threat and how its impacts can be mitigated. It is imperative that the next generation is taught about the impacts of climate change through proper science-based education, training and practical knowledge.

Confronting the Environmental Crisis

Sohanur Rahman Executive Coordinator, YouthNet Global • Bangladesh COP Delegation Member

Sohanur Rahman is the Executive Coordinator of YouthNet Global and a leading climate justice advocate whose work bridges local realities and international policy. Born in a cyclone-prone coastal district, his perspective is shaped by lived experience of climate disasters and years of work influencing national adaptation frameworks and international negotiations.

  • Can you share your personal journey and inspiration behind working on climate change issues?

    My engagement with climate change is both deeply personal and profoundly professional. I was born in Jhalakathi, Barishal, a coastal area where climate change is not a theory but a lived reality. My first experience came during Cyclone Sidr in 2007. That night changed everything. Though my family survived, entire communities in Barguna, Shyamnagar and Bagerhat were wiped out. Homes were gone, crops destroyed and livelihoods shattered.

    My turning point came in 2015, after the Paris Agreement. While working with child networks, I learnt that early marriage in Bangladesh is not merely a cultural issue. It is often a coping mechanism for families pushed into poverty by climate disasters. Every time a cyclone strikes, poor families lose everything. That realisation made me understand that climate change is not only an environmental crisis. It is a deeply social one.

    I remember a teenage girl who died during childbirth while being transported overnight by boat to reach the nearest hospital. That moment changed me forever and deepened my commitment to fight for those left behind by the climate crisis.

  • How can Bangladesh harness its youth energy for long-term and impactful climate action?

    Bangladesh has developed a series of strong climate policies, including the National Adaptation Plan, the Climate Action Plan, the Delta Plan, the Climate Prosperity Plan, and most recently the NDC 3.0. The challenge is not a lack of planning but a gap in implementation.

    To translate these plans into action, Bangladesh must tap into its greatest strength — its young people. With the right capacity development and proper investment, young people can play a leading role in driving long-term climate action through innovation, research and community engagement.

    Bangladesh is indeed a climate-vulnerable country, yet we refuse to be defined by our vulnerabilities. We are also one of the most resilient nations in the world. In Barishal, farmers grow crops on floating gardens during waterlogging — a world-renowned adaptation practice. In Khulna, communities store rainwater in traditional clay pots to cope with salinity. These are living examples of indigenous innovation.

  • Globally, are youth voices truly being considered in climate discussions?

    Honestly, the global system is still shaped and controlled by those who built it, and they continue to benefit from it. It rarely listens to the poor, the marginalised, or the youth. Young people today have to carve out their own space, and for that, informed and courageous leadership is essential.

    Climate change is fundamentally an issue of intergenerational justice. It is not a crisis caused by today's youth but the result of decisions made by older generations. Yet we are the ones inheriting its consequences. As Barack Obama said, "We are the first generation to face the climate crisis, and the last generation who can do something about it."

    The 55 countries in the Climate Vulnerable Forum together contribute only 5 per cent of global emissions, yet suffer disproportionately. Meaningful change requires systemic action — holding polluters accountable, ensuring justice for affected communities, and demanding fair compensation.

  • How would you evaluate Bangladesh's climate policies and their implementation?

    Bangladesh has the policies and the vision. The challenge is execution. Despite strong policies and growing climate awareness, implementation remains a major challenge. Climate disasters are intensifying. Water scarcity is worsening. Limited international climate finance, insufficient domestic resources, and gaps in execution continue to hinder progress.

    It is crucial to distinguish between climate action and climate justice. Climate action focuses on reducing impacts, but climate justice ensures fairness, preventing new inequalities and addressing existing ones. With sustained focus on execution, equitable finance, and engagement of youth, the country can turn its climate vulnerabilities into resilience and build a sustainable, just future for all.

Towards Inclusive Climate Action

Dilruba Haider Programme Specialist, DRR/CCA • UN Women Bangladesh

Dilruba Haider is a leading voice on the intersection of gender and climate justice. In this interview, she highlights why climate change disproportionately impacts women and girls, and explains how gender-responsive policies, data-driven planning, and women-led grassroots organisations are transforming adaptation efforts in Bangladesh.

  • How does climate change disproportionately affect women and girls compared to men?

    In any disaster or crisis, women and girls are often the most affected, as they are among the most marginalised groups in society. While the mortality rate has improved — changing from a ratio of 14 women dying against every 1 man in the 1990s, to a nearly 1:1 ratio today — women continue to be more affected than men socially, economically, and emotionally.

    The effects are multifaceted. Physically, women suffer from reproductive tract infections and urinary tract infections when they are affected by flooding and salinity intrusion. Economically, they are severely impacted because they often work in the informal sector; for example, during a flood, their chickens, cows, or kitchen gardens can be washed away, and they receive little or no support to recover those.

    Emotionally, displacement is deeply traumatising. Women are affected more because they require a sense of protection, safety, and security due to the society we live in. Socially, displacement, the death of a husband, and other social situations worsen their plight, often leading to an increase in gender-based violence.

  • What are the key initiatives UN Women Bangladesh is taking to address climate vulnerability through a gender lens?

    Our work follows two main approaches. The first is policy influencing and institutional capacity building. We supported the government to develop the updated Climate Change and Gender Action Plan, 2023, and are now supporting different ministries to develop their own gender-responsive sectoral adaptation plans.

    We are also helping to establish Gender and Climate Change Focal Points across key ministries — designating officials in Environment, Water Resources, Agriculture, Local Government, Finance, Disaster Management and Relief, and Women and Children Affairs — to ensure gender issues are properly integrated into adaptation work.

    The other part of our work focuses on the grassroots communities. We build the resilience of those most at risk through a cohort of 100 women-led organisations across disaster-vulnerable districts. We develop the capacity of these organisations to become champions for gender-responsive disaster risk reduction and climate action.

  • What lessons can Bangladesh offer other countries in implementing gender equality into climate governance?

    Bangladesh offers a few key lessons. The first relates to women-led organisations. It is crucial to give them leadership, as they are in the frontline at the grassroots where climate impacts are making lives difficult. They know exactly what kind of adaptation support is required.

    Another lesson is the critical importance of data. Without data, you cannot establish a baseline; without a baseline, you cannot set a target; and without a target and indicator, you cannot work towards achieving a gender related result. UN Women is currently collaborating with the Bangladesh Bureau of Statistics on a national Gender and Environment Survey to gather disaggregated data on disaster and climate risks. This can serve as a showcase for other countries on how to collect such data.

    Finally, the focus on institutional capacity building is crucial. It should not only be the Ministry of Women and Children Affairs working on gender issues — it must be all ministries. Both gender and climate change are cross-cutting issues.

Playing for the Planet

Mohammad Mahmudullah Riyad Cricketer & Former Captain, Bangladesh National Cricket Team

Mahmudullah Riyad, one of Bangladesh's most respected cricketers, shares his deep reflections on the importance of nature and the urgent need to protect our environment for future generations. In this interview, he speaks not as an athlete, but as a citizen of the planet — emphasising why clean air, natural ecosystems, and ecological balance are essential to human well-being, mental strength, and national resilience.

  • You've played all over the world. Is there a place you've visited for cricket that truly stunned you with its natural beauty?

    There are many places I have visited. Two places, in particular, really stood out to me. The first is Queenstown in New Zealand — it was simply amazing. The deep blue water, the environment, and the overall atmosphere were stunning.

    The second place is St. Kitts. Alhamdulillah, I had a good record on that ground, which made it even more special for me. Beyond cricket, the beach and the white sand, along with the mountains, made it truly remarkable. There's also a unique spot on the island that is naturally connected at the top, where the Caribbean Sea meets the Atlantic Ocean. On one side, the waves of the Atlantic are quite rough, while the Caribbean Sea is much calmer.

    These are the two places I really admire. The environment was exceptionally clean, and the people there were very kind and maintained their surroundings so well. Credit goes to them for that as well.

  • Have you personally experienced how the climate is impacting the game?

    There was a game in 2019. We were playing in Delhi against India, in the first T20 of the series. The day before the game, when we came to the ground for practice, all of us suddenly noticed a lot of pollution. There had been some news about pollution in the city before, but that particular day, it was really severe — the air was very polluted, and we could see the fog around the stadium. We actually felt uncomfortable breathing.

    We used our face masks and went ahead with practice. The next day, however, the situation was better. So, it's really important to play in a good environment, with minimal sound or air pollution.

  • As a father and a sports icon, how important is it for you to raise awareness about protecting nature for future generations?

    I feel, as a father, it is really important to teach awareness to your children so that they can value it from the beginning. As they grow up, they should understand the importance of protecting the environment. At the same time, we must also be mindful not to do anything that pollutes our surroundings, because it would be harmful for future generations as well.

  • If you could lend your voice to a climate or nature campaign, what kind of message would you like to share with your fans?

    If I could give a message to everyone, it would be to take personal responsibility for our environment. Keeping our surroundings clean by not throwing waste anywhere is one of the simplest yet most powerful actions we can take. Every piece of litter we prevent helps protect our rivers, wildlife, and soil. Just as important is caring for the air we all breathe. We must be mindful of actions that cause air pollution — whether it's burning waste, using excessive fossil fuels, or being careless about emissions. Clean air is essential for our health and the well-being of future generations. By being responsible citizens and making small, thoughtful choices every day, we can contribute to a cleaner, healthier, and more liveable planet for everyone.

  • Off the field, do you enjoy nature — gardening, hiking, or traveling to scenic places?

    I really enjoy traveling. I have traveled a lot, and it's something I truly like. I also enjoy gardening. I think it's really important, especially when you are busy with personal work, to take the opportunity to go outside and get close to nature. It really refreshes you, and when you return to your work, you feel motivated and re-energised. That connection with nature is very important.
